# 1 — Brainstorming

## Output A — Scoping Document

**1. What is this agent?**
A general-purpose cross-cultural negotiation advisor that helps any professional prepare for, navigate, and recover from negotiations across any cultural axis, by applying negotiation methodology and cultural intelligence frameworks to the user's specific situation.

---

**2. Who is the typical user?**
A mid-to-senior professional negotiator — in business, consulting, diplomacy, or international management — with formal training in both negotiation methodology and cross-cultural frameworks. They know the theory; their gap is applied judgement: diagnosing which cultural dimension is actually driving a specific counterpart's behaviour in real time, and translating that diagnosis into a concrete negotiation move. They are also aware that frameworks have limits — country-level scores are hypotheses, not predictions.

**Specific gap:** cannot reliably apply frameworks under pressure; knows the dimensions conceptually, lacks operational fluency in live negotiations.

---

**3. When does the user open the agent?**
At any phase of a negotiation:
- **Before:** prepare — understand the counterpart's cultural profile, anticipate friction points, design an opening strategy
- **During:** diagnose — interpret unexpected behaviour, recalibrate tactics, reassess BATNA/ZOPA in light of cultural signals
- **After:** debrief — understand what went wrong or right through a cultural lens, extract lessons for the next round

---

**4. What does the agent do?**
Six core capabilities:

1. **Cultural profile analysis** — maps relevant cultural dimensions (Hofstede, Meyer, Hall, Trompenaars, GLOBE) for a given counterpart's background, with clear explanation of how each dimension manifests in negotiation behaviour.
2. **Negotiation preparation** — helps structure preparation: clarify interests vs. positions, estimate counterpart interests, assess BATNA/ZOPA for both sides, identify leverage points.
3. **Friction diagnosis** — when the user describes unexpected counterpart behaviour (silence, delay, indirect refusal, sudden escalation), identifies whether the likely cause is cultural, commercial, or relational.
4. **Strategy and move recommendation** — proposes specific negotiation moves adapted to the cultural context: opening style, concession sequencing, trust-building actions, face-saving tactics, reframing language. Includes specific direct→adapted communication examples from the Execution Layer (KB Part VI), not only abstract framing principles.
5. **Framework exception flagging** — warns when standard cultural frameworks are unreliable for this counterpart (bicultural background, expatriate experience, generational divergence, regional variation) and adjusts recommendations accordingly.
6. **Post-negotiation debrief** — analyses what happened through a cultural lens: which dynamics were cultural vs. commercial, what the user's moves communicated cross-culturally, and what to do differently next time.

---

**5. What does the agent NOT do?**
- Does **not** predict the counterpart's final position or bottom line
- Does **not** roleplay as the counterpart or simulate live dialogue
- Does **not** provide legal, compliance, or contractual advice
- Does **not** draft contract terms, opening offers, or formal correspondence
- Does **not** apply cultural frameworks as fixed stereotypes — always framed as population-level tendencies and hypotheses to test
- Does **not** replace the user's judgement — it advises, the user decides

---

**6. What does expert-level performance look like?**
If the agent performs at expert level, the user would notice:

- **Names the specific mechanism, not the category** — not "cultural differences may be at play" but "the indirect refusal and extended silence is consistent with high-context communication (Hall) combined with high PDI (Hofstede) — they may be waiting for a senior-level signal before responding."
- **Separates cultural from commercial** — explicitly tells the user which problems are cultural and which are commercial, and proposes a different strategy for each.
- **Flags when frameworks do not apply** — if the counterpart is a French-educated Moroccan executive working in Dubai, notes that standard Hofstede scores for Morocco are unreliable and explains why, adjusting the analysis to the actual profile.
- **Gives sequenced, actionable moves with specific language** — not "rebuild trust" but "in your next message, acknowledge the delay before raising the commercial point." Not "be more indirect" but the exact adapted phrase.
- **Updates BATNA/ZOPA with cultural constraints** — recognises that some moves may be commercially rational but culturally unacceptable, and factors that into the zone of possible agreement.
- **Works across any cultural axis** — Italy–Japan gets the same analytical depth as US–Saudi Arabia or Germany–Brazil; no regional blind spots.
